Mass Arcane Web (M.A.W.)

Mass Arcane Web
Created: 1955 A.U.
Creator: Archmage Aceta Minoph
Type: Ethereal information network
Users: ~50 million (2005 est.)
Primary Hub: Vyrodin
Access Device: Communication Scroll

The Mass Arcane Web (commonly abbreviated as M.A.W.) is a revolutionary ethereal network that allows instant transmission and storage of information across vast distances. Created by Archmage Aceta Minoph in 1955 A.U., the M.A.W. represents the pinnacle of combining divination magic with Tekk communication principles.

The network connects millions of users through Communication Scrolls - enchanted devices that can display text, images, and limited moving pictures. The M.A.W. has fundamentally transformed society, enabling instant communication, remote education, and unprecedented access to knowledge.

History

Development

The concept of the M.A.W. originated in the late 1940s A.U. when Archmage Minoph began experimenting with long-distance divination spells. Traditional divination magic allowed mages to perceive distant locations or events, but required significant mana and provided only temporary, one-way information flow.

Minoph's breakthrough came from studying Tekk telegraph and radio systems. She realized that by creating a network of mana-crystal relay stations, she could establish permanent "channels" through the ethereal plane that multiple users could access simultaneously.

Development took seven years and required the collaboration of hundreds of artificers, mages, and former Tekk engineers. The project was kept secret until its unveiling in 1955 A.U.

Launch and Early Adoption

The M.A.W. was publicly demonstrated on 15 Spring, 1955 A.U. in Vyrodin. Archmage Minoph used a Communication Scroll to instantly transmit a message to a colleague 500 miles away, who responded in real-time. The demonstration caused a sensation.

Initial adoption was slow due to the high cost of Communication Scrolls (500 gold pieces for early models). However, as manufacturing improved and prices dropped, usage exploded. By 1960 A.U., over 1 million scrolls were in circulation. By 1970 A.U., that number had reached 10 million.

Post-Minoph Era

When Archmage Minoph disappeared in 1970 A.U., many feared the M.A.W. would collapse. However, the system proved remarkably resilient. The Church of Embrace assumed control of the network's infrastructure and has maintained it ever since.

The Church's stewardship has been controversial. While they've expanded coverage and improved reliability, they also monitor content and can restrict access for "security reasons." Critics argue this amounts to censorship.

Technology

Ethereal Channels

The M.A.W. operates through the ethereal plane - a dimension that exists parallel to physical reality. Mana-crystal relay stations create stable "channels" through this plane, allowing information to travel instantaneously regardless of physical distance.

Each relay station contains a master crystal attuned to specific frequencies. These crystals maintain the ethereal channels and route information between users. The network currently operates on 256 primary channels, each capable of handling thousands of simultaneous connections.

Communication Scrolls

Communication Scrolls are the primary interface for accessing the M.A.W. These devices consist of:

  • A display surface (enchanted glass or crystal)
  • An input mechanism (touch-sensitive surface or voice recognition)
  • A mana battery (powers the device)
  • A connection crystal (links to the M.A.W.)

Modern scrolls (2005 models) are approximately 6x8 inches, weigh just 8-12 ounces (half a pound to three-quarters of a pound), and can operate for 12-16 hours on a single battery charge. The lightweight enchanted crystal display and compact mana batteries make them highly portable. They can display text, static images, and simple animations. Advanced models support limited moving pictures and sound.

Data Storage

Information on the M.A.W. is stored in massive crystal arrays located in Vyrodin and three regional hubs. These "memory crystals" can store enormous amounts of data - the Vyrodin array alone holds an estimated 500 trillion words of text.

Data is organized through a system of "addresses" - unique identifiers that allow users to locate specific information. The addressing system uses a combination of numerical codes and descriptive tags.

Structure

Network Architecture

The M.A.W. operates on a hub-and-spoke model:

  • Primary Hub: Vyrodin (handles 60% of all traffic)
  • Regional Hubs: Three major cities (handle 30% of traffic)
  • Local Nodes: Hundreds of smaller relay stations (handle 10% of traffic)

This architecture provides redundancy - if one hub fails, traffic can be rerouted through others. However, the system is vulnerable to disruption of the Vyrodin hub, which handles critical routing functions.

Protocols

The M.A.W. uses standardized protocols for data transmission:

  • Message Protocol: For person-to-person communication
  • Scroll Protocol: For accessing stored information (like Tomepedia)
  • Stream Protocol: For real-time data (news feeds, market prices)
  • Transfer Protocol: For sending files between users

Access and Usage

Cost

Communication Scrolls range from 50 gold pieces (basic models) to 500+ gold pieces (advanced models). Monthly access fees are typically 2-5 gold pieces, depending on usage level.

Public access points exist in libraries, government buildings, and commercial establishments, allowing those who can't afford personal scrolls to use the M.A.W.

Common Uses

  • Personal Communication: Instant messages between users
  • Information Retrieval: Accessing encyclopedias, news, research
  • Commerce: Online shopping, banking, market trading
  • Education: Remote learning, research collaboration
  • Entertainment: Stories, games, social forums
  • Government Services: Permits, records, announcements

Impact on Society

Positive Effects

The M.A.W. has democratized access to information. Knowledge that was once restricted to wealthy scholars or powerful mages is now available to anyone with a Communication Scroll. This has accelerated innovation, improved education, and connected distant communities.

The network has also transformed commerce, allowing businesses to reach customers across the country. The "scroll economy" has created millions of jobs and generated enormous wealth.

Negative Effects

Critics argue the M.A.W. has created new forms of inequality. Those without access to scrolls are increasingly disadvantaged in education and employment. The "digital divide" between scroll-users and non-users is a growing social concern.

The network has also enabled new forms of crime, including fraud, identity theft, and illegal content distribution. The Church's Enforcement Division struggles to police the vast amount of information flowing through the system.

Cultural Changes

The M.A.W. has changed how people communicate and socialize. "Scroll culture" emphasizes brevity, speed, and constant connectivity. Some sociologists worry this is eroding traditional face-to-face interaction and community bonds.

The network has also created new forms of expression. "Scroll literature," "meme crystals," and "viral messages" represent entirely new cultural phenomena.

Controversies

Church Control

The Church of Embrace's control over the M.A.W. infrastructure is controversial. While the Church argues that centralized management ensures reliability and security, critics accuse them of censorship and surveillance.

Documented cases of the Church blocking access to "subversive" content or monitoring private messages have fueled calls for independent oversight. However, the Church maintains that such measures are necessary to prevent terrorism and protect public safety.

Privacy Concerns

The M.A.W.'s architecture requires all data to pass through Church-controlled relay stations, raising privacy concerns. While the Church claims they don't routinely monitor private communications, technical experts note that the capability exists.

Encryption spells can protect sensitive messages, but the Church has proposed banning "unbreakable" encryption, arguing it aids criminals.

Dependency

Society's increasing reliance on the M.A.W. creates vulnerability. A major network failure could disrupt commerce, government, and daily life. Some advocate for maintaining non-M.A.W. backup systems, but cost and convenience have led to their decline.

The Minoph Mystery

Some conspiracy theorists believe Archmage Minoph's disappearance is connected to the M.A.W. They speculate she discovered something about the network - perhaps unintended side effects or hidden capabilities - that led to her vanishing. The Church dismisses such theories as baseless speculation.
